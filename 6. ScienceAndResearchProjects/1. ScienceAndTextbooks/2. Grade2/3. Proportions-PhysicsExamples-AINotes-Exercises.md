Direct and Indirect Proportions in Physics
==========================================

If you want to understand how the universe works, you have to understand how things change in relation to each other. In physics, we usually describe these relationships using **proportions**.

Think of it like a "buy one, get one" deal versus a "sharing a pizza" situation. Here is the breakdown of the two main types.

1. Direct Proportion: "The Follower"

------------------------------------

In a direct proportion, two things are in sync. If one goes up, the other goes up. If one is cut in half, the other is cut in half. They follow each other at a constant rate.

### The Symbol and the Equation

Before we write a full equation, we use a special symbol to show two things are related: $\propto$. This symbol means "is proportional to."

To turn that relationship into a mathematical equation ($=$), we introduce a **constant (**$k$**)**. This $k$ represents the "rule" or the "stiffness" of the relationship.

* **Relationship:** $y \propto x$

* **Equation:** $y = k \cdot x$

### Physics Example A: Stretching a Spring (Hooke’s Law)

Imagine you have a spring hanging from the ceiling. You hang a small weight on it, and it stretches a little. You hang a heavier weight, and it stretches more.

* **The Physics:** The force ($F$) you apply is directly proportional to the extension ($x$).

* **The Equation:** $F = k \cdot x$
  
  * $F$ is Force (the weight).
  
  * $k$ is the "Spring Constant" (how stiff the spring is).
  
  * $x$ is the extension (how far it stretched).

* **Numerical Example:** Suppose you have a spring with a constant $k = 10$.
  
  1. If you pull with $10\text{ Newtons}$, it stretches $1\text{ cm}$ ($10 = 10 \cdot 1$).
  
  2. If you **double** the force to $20\text{ Newtons}$, it stretches $2\text{ cm}$ ($20 = 10 \cdot 2$).
  
  3. If you use $50\text{ Newtons}$, it stretches $5\text{ cm}$ ($50 = 10 \cdot 5$). _Notice how the ratio_ $F/x$ _always equals_ $10$_._

### Physics Example B: Electricity (Ohm’s Law)

**Voltage** is the "pressure" pushing the electricity, and **Current** is how much electricity is actually flowing.

* **The Physics:** Current is directly proportional to Voltage.

* **The Equation:** $V = I \cdot R$
  
  * $V$ is Voltage.
  
  * $I$ is Current.
  
  * $R$ is **Resistance** (the constant in this case).

* **Numerical Example:** Suppose you have a lightbulb with a resistance $R = 5\text{ Ohms}$.
  
  1. Push with $10\text{ Volts} \rightarrow$ Current is $2\text{ Amps}$ ($10 = 2 \cdot 5$).
  
  2. Push with $20\text{ Volts} \rightarrow$ Current is $4\text{ Amps}$ ($20 = 4 \cdot 5$). _Double the voltage, double the current._
2. Indirect (Inverse) Proportion: "The Seesaw"

----------------------------------------------

In an indirect proportion, things move in opposite directions. If one goes up, the other goes down. It’s like a seesaw—when one side rises, the other side must drop.

### The Symbol and the Equation

To show an indirect relationship, we use the same symbol but put the second variable at the bottom of a fraction. To make it an equation, we again use our constant $k$.

* **Relationship:** $y \propto \frac{1}{x}$

* **Equation:** $y = \frac{k}{x} \quad \text{or} \quad x \cdot y = k$

This means that no matter how $x$ and $y$ change, when you multiply them together, you always get the same number ($k$).

### Physics Example C: Pushing a Heavy Object (Newton’s 2nd Law)

For a constant force, acceleration is inversely proportional to mass.

* **The Equation:** $a = \frac{F}{m}$
  
  * $F$ is the constant Force you are pushing with.
  
  * $m$ is the Mass.
  
  * $a$ is the Acceleration.

* **Numerical Example:** Imagine you are pushing with a constant force of $100\text{ Newtons}$.
  
  1. Push a $10\text{ kg}$ cart $\rightarrow$ Acceleration is $10\text{ m/s}^2$ ($100 / 10 = 10$).
  
  2. Push a **heavier** $20\text{ kg}$ cart $\rightarrow$ Acceleration drops to $5\text{ m/s}^2$ ($100 / 20 = 5$).
  
  3. Push a **massive** $100\text{ kg}$ cart $\rightarrow$ Acceleration is only $1\text{ m/s}^2$ ($100 / 100 = 1$).

### Physics Example D: Squeezing a Balloon (Boyle’s Law)

The pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

* **The Equation:** $P \cdot V = k$
  
  * $P$ is Pressure.
  
  * $V$ is Volume.

* **Numerical Example:** Suppose you have a container where the "magic constant" $k$ is $12$.
  
  1. Volume is $4\text{ Liters} \rightarrow$ Pressure must be $3$ ($4 \cdot 3 = 12$).
  
  2. Squeeze it to $2\text{ Liters}$ (half the space) $\rightarrow$ Pressure jumps to $6$ ($2 \cdot 6 = 12$).
  
  3. Squeeze it to $1\text{ Liter}$ $\rightarrow$ Pressure is now $12$ ($1 \cdot 12 = 12$). _As volume gets smaller, pressure gets bigger._

Summary Table
-------------

| Relationship Type | Symbol                  | Generic Equation  | Constant ($k$) role              | Graph Shape                   |
| ----------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| **Direct**        | $y \propto x$           | $y = k \cdot x$   | The slope/steepness.             | Straight line through origin. |
| **Indirect**      | $y \propto \frac{1}{x}$ | $y = \frac{k}{x}$ | The fixed product ($x \cdot y$). | A dropping curve (Hyperbola). |

### Pro-Tip for Physics:

Whenever you see an equation, look for the variables. If they are on opposite sides of the equals sign and both on top (like $F$ and $x$ in $F=kx$), they are **direct**. If they are on the same side being multiplied (like $P$ and $V$ in $PV=k$), they are **indirect**!
Practice Exercises
------------------

### Section 1: Direct Proportions (Springs & Electricity)

1. A spring has a constant $k = 15$. If you pull it with $45\text{ Newtons}$, how many centimeters will it stretch?

2. If a $20\text{ Newton}$ force stretches a spring $4\text{ cm}$, what is the spring constant $k$?

3. Using the same spring from Question 2, how much force is needed to stretch it $10\text{ cm}$?

4. A circuit has a resistance ($R$) of $12\text{ Ohms}$. If the current ($I$) is $3\text{ Amps}$, what is the voltage ($V$)?

5. If you triple the voltage in a circuit while keeping the resistance the same, what happens to the current?

6. You measure $6\text{ Amps}$ of current flowing through a $4\text{ Ohm}$ resistor. If the resistance stays the same but the current drops to $2\text{ Amps}$, what is the new voltage?

7. A spring stretches $2\text{ cm}$ for every $5\text{ Newtons}$ added. Create a small table showing the extension for $10\text{ N}$, $15\text{ N}$, and $20\text{ N}$.

8. In Ohm’s Law ($V = IR$), if you want to keep the current ($I$) exactly the same but you double the resistance ($R$), what must you do to the voltage ($V$)?

9. If $y \propto x$ and $y=24$ when $x=3$, find the constant $k$ and the value of $y$ when $x=10$.

10. Why does a direct proportion graph always have to pass through the point $(0,0)$? Explain using the spring example.

### Section 2: Indirect Proportions (Motion & Gases)

11. You push an object with a constant force. If the mass is $5\text{ kg}$ and the acceleration is $20\text{ m/s}^2$, what is the force ($k$)?

12. Using the same force from Question 11, if the mass increases to $10\text{ kg}$, what is the new acceleration?

13. A gas has a volume of $10\text{ Liters}$ at a pressure of $2\text{ atmospheres}$. What is the constant $k$?

14. If you compress the gas from Question 13 until the volume is only $4\text{ Liters}$, what will the new pressure be?

15. If $y \propto 1/x$ and $y=10$ when $x=2$, what is the constant $k$? What is $y$ when $x=5$?

16. On a "seesaw" relationship, if you cut the mass of an object in half, what happens to its acceleration (assuming the force stays constant)?

17. A diver bubbles out air underwater. As the bubble rises, the pressure decreases. According to Boyle's Law, what should happen to the size (volume) of the bubble?

18. You are pushing a cart. You want it to accelerate 4 times faster than it is now, but you can't change your pushing force. How much mass would you need to remove from the cart?

19. Look at the equation $P = k/V$. If the volume $V$ becomes extremely large (close to infinity), what happens to the pressure $P$?

20. Explain the difference between the "constant" in a direct proportion equation ($y/x = k$) versus an indirect one ($xy = k$).
